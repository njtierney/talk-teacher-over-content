Time >>> content

  • time, not content, is the bottleneck for learning
  • The learner carves out time: protects it from distraction
  • The learner gets YOUR time as the teacher
  • They get to ask an expert, clarify misunderstandings
    • “How did you do that?”
    • “Why this way over XXX?”
    • “Is that the same as YYY?”

Course ideas are important and worthwhile

I think the two most important messages that people can get from a short course are:

  1. the material is important and worthwhile to learn (even if it’s challenging), and
  2. it’s possible to learn it!

– Hadley Wickham - from a github gist in 2015

The online materials continue to support that learning

  • No searching through emails to find PDFs of slides
  • Course materials are a living document that will be up to date
  • This material adds to the narrative, provides help in the future
  • Deepens your own understanding of the topic: built-in professional development

How can “free” be sustainable?

  • Courses aren’t a primary product/source of income
  • I view courses and teaching as a way to get people in front of you
  • Demonstrates your expertise - show people what you know
    • This is “Free advertising”
    • These could be future clients

What do people pay for then?

  • I am not saying: “Deliver your courses for free”
  • I am saying: People pay for their time in your course
  • I am saying: give people an option
  • If they want to put in the extra work, use your materials as a resource, to learn the thing, great
  • If they want to see you “in person” (or on screen?), then they can pay for that
  • But putting the course material up online kind of only really serves to help you
  • If students/users are given a packet of slides or teaching materials, then if they return to them in the following weeks/months/years, then are they really going to be kept up to date?
  • How do you find your teaching materials again? Is it in an email? What if you don’t have access to that email address again?
  • Make your teaching material accessible - put it on github, on your website, on zenodo. Put it online.
  • Congratulations, your teaching resource has now transformed into

How to: put a course online

  • Make a bookdown/quarto book
  • Add an appropriate license CC BY-NC-4.0
    • You can share/adapt
    • You must attribute
    • You cannot use the material for commercial purposes
  • Put it on github
  • Have it render when you make changes
  • Deliver the course in-person, or online
  • You don’t need to share “course notes” or slides with your students
  • They just get everything from the online book

To build a sustainable consulting practice, I think you need a genuine value-add to the wider community. I am not convinced writing a post such as “10 things you need to do now to improve your data visualisations” cuts it. Teaching does. And more, I think you should share your course materials for free.

In this talk I will argue teaching and openly sharing your materials is a sustainable business model. The value learners pay for when they attend a course is not the slides or the code, those should be freely available online. The value they get, which they are paying for, is time with the instructor, and the commitment they make to carving out space to learn something new.

Developing free and open content compounds over time. It creates a living document you can refer to and update. It demonstrates your expertise not by telling people you know things, but by showing them. It surfaces your work to learners who would never have found you otherwise, and those learners become the pipeline for future consulting and collaboration. It also deepens your own understanding of the topic. So, you get professional development built in.

I will discuss a case study in R training: Quarto for Scientists to illustrate how this works in practice. I hope to leave attendees with the conviction that creating useful, openly available learning content is not a distraction from building a consulting practice, but is a highly effective way to build, and sustain one.
